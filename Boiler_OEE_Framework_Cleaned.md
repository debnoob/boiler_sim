# Beyond Availability, Performance, and Quality: A Framework for Boiler Overall Energy Effectiveness in Industrial Automation Platforms

## Conceptual Shift: Adapting OEE for Continuous-Process Boiler Systems

The traditional OEE framework, rooted in discrete manufacturing, is predicated on the production of individual, identifiable units (e.g., widgets, bottles) per unit of time. Boilers, however, are continuous-process assets whose primary function is to transform energy from a fuel source into thermal energy contained within steam or hot water. They do not produce discrete "units"; instead, they generate a continuous flow of a medium (steam) that serves as an energy carrier for subsequent processes. This fundamental difference renders a direct application of the standard Availability × Performance × Quality formula impractical and conceptually misleading.

Therefore, adapting OEE for boiler systems requires a paradigm shift: moving the analytical focus from measuring the loss of productive time in terms of units per hour to measuring the loss of energy and its efficient conversion.

In this adapted model, the core OEE components are re-interpreted to align with the operational goals and key performance indicators (KPIs) of a boiler system. The goal is no longer to maximize the number of widgets produced per hour, but to maximize the amount of usable thermal energy produced per unit of fuel consumed, while maintaining stable and reliable operation.

First, Availability remains the most readily transferable concept. It continues to measure the percentage of time the boiler is available to produce steam according to schedule. This is a critical metric for ensuring uninterrupted steam supply to downstream processes, which is vital for the stability of many industrial operations. Losses in availability are still attributed to downtime from unplanned events like mechanical failures, burner malfunctions, or control system issues, as well as planned events such as scheduled maintenance shutdowns or extended cleaning periods. The calculation formula, **Availability = Run Time / Planned Production Time**, remains structurally sound, though "run time" now signifies the duration the boiler is actively generating steam at or above a minimum threshold, rather than simply being powered on.

Second, the Performance component undergoes a significant transformation. In discrete manufacturing, performance loss is measured in reduced cycles per minute or short stops. For a boiler, this translates into inefficiencies in the combustion and heat transfer processes. The analogue to Performance is therefore Thermal Efficiency or Energy Efficiency. This metric quantifies how effectively the boiler converts the chemical energy stored in the fuel into usable thermal energy in the steam. It reflects the combined performance of the furnace, heat exchanger surfaces, and the combustion process itself. High efficiency means less fuel is wasted, leading to direct cost savings and reduced environmental impact. Boiler efficiency can be calculated using either the Direct Method (measuring energy output versus fuel input) or the Indirect Method (calculating major heat losses). Case studies demonstrate that boiler efficiency can be a highly variable and critical parameter; one study reported a boiler efficiency of 53.67% due to high exhaust gas and radiation losses, while another showed a rice mill improving its thermal efficiency to about 78% through energy-efficient upgrades. Further optimization efforts have been shown to improve boiler efficiency from 80% to 83.75% by addressing issues like unstable combustion and high stack oxygen levels. Thus, the adapted Performance factor becomes a measure of how close the boiler is running to its maximum possible thermal efficiency.

Third, the Quality component is also redefined. In a discrete context, quality is binary: a part is either good or bad. For a boiler, "quality" refers to the fitness-for-purpose of the steam it produces. This is determined by its adherence to specified parameters such as correct pressure, temperature, and dryness fraction. A "quality" steam output is stable, consistent, and meets the setpoints required by the end-user processes. Instability, such as frequent cycling or pressure/temperature fluctuations, can be considered a quality loss as it degrades the performance of downstream equipment and can lead to process disruptions. Furthermore, emissions compliance can be integrated into the Quality component. Modern regulations require strict control over pollutants like NOx and CO. Operating outside of optimal combustion parameters to save fuel might lead to excessive emissions, constituting a quality failure against regulatory standards. Therefore, the Quality factor in a boiler's adapted OEE model could be a proxy for operational stability and adherence to both process and environmental specifications.

Based on these adapted components, a practical formula for a boiler-specific OEE, which can be conceptualized as Boiler Overall Energy Effectiveness (BOEE), emerges. One source provides an explicit model for such a calculation using an Excel workbook, which uses operational uptime/downtime data and efficiency data as its primary inputs. In this model, the efficiency is calculated as a percentage relative to the rated boiler efficiency, which is determined by measured temperature and pressure. This demonstrates a viable hybrid approach that combines availability with thermal performance. A generalized version of this adapted formula would be:

$$\text{BOEE} = \text{Availability} \times \text{Thermal Efficiency} \times \text{Steam Quality Factor}$$

Where:
- **Availability** is the uptime percentage.
- **Thermal Efficiency** is the boiler's current efficiency as a percentage of its rated or optimal efficiency.
- **Steam Quality Factor** is a metric reflecting the stability and conformity of steam parameters (pressure, temperature).

To calculate the Thermal Efficiency component, engineers rely on several key boiler-specific metrics that serve as direct inputs. The Steam-to-Fuel Ratio (S:F) is a powerful and intuitive indicator of boiler performance, measuring the kilograms of steam generated per kilogram of fuel consumed. A higher S:F ratio signifies better economic performance. One case study documented a significant improvement in boiler efficiency from 80% to 83.75% after optimizing combustion controls, which led to an increase in the S:F ratio from 4.7 kg/kg to 5.5 kg/kg—a 17% reduction in fuel consumption for the same steam output. This metric directly links operational adjustments to tangible financial savings.

Another crucial set of metrics revolves around Combustion Efficiency. This is intrinsically linked to the air-to-fuel ratio. Maintaining an optimal mixture is paramount for maximizing thermal efficiency and minimizing unburnt residues in the ash and harmful emissions. Honeywell's combustion safety solutions explicitly address controlling the air/fuel ratio in burner systems, highlighting its importance. Monitoring stack oxygen levels is a common method for assessing combustion efficiency; high oxygen levels indicate excess air, which cools the furnace and carries away valuable heat in the flue gases, reducing overall efficiency. Optimizing this ratio, for instance by trimming oxygen levels, is identified as an efficient method for improving boiler efficiency without major plant modifications. The solution implemented at a pharmaceutical plant involved coordinated fuel-air control to maintain optimal O₂ levels between 4-9%, which contributed to the improved efficiency and stability.

Finally, Heat Rate is another important measure of boiler performance, representing the amount of fuel energy required to produce a unit of steam energy. A lower heat rate indicates a more efficient boiler, as less fuel is needed to generate the same amount of steam. The largest single heat loss in most boilers is from the exhaust gases, making the management of flue gas volume and temperature a key lever for improving efficiency. Other factors contributing to heat loss include radiation and convection losses from the boiler vessel, moisture in the fuel, and unburnt residues in the ashes.

In summary, the adaptation of OEE for boiler systems is not merely a matter of substituting new names for old variables. It represents a fundamental shift in analytical thinking, from counting discrete units to tracking the flow and conversion of energy. The resulting BOEE score, while retaining the familiar multiplicative structure, gains entirely new meaning. It becomes a holistic health score for the boiler asset, reflecting its ability to reliably produce high-quality steam energy as efficiently as possible. This adapted framework provides a far more relevant and actionable assessment of boiler performance than a naive application of the discrete manufacturing model would allow.

## Implementation in Rockwell Automation Ecosystems

Rockwell Automation provides a comprehensive, multi-layered ecosystem of hardware and software tools designed to facilitate advanced process control, data management, and analytics. When examining the implementation of boiler-specific OEE calculations within this environment, a clear strategy emerges that combines pre-packaged functional blocks with robust IT/OT convergence platforms for data contextualization and reporting. This approach offers a structured path for engineers to implement the adapted boiler OEE methodology.

At the heart of the control layer is Rockwell's PlantPAx distributed control system (DCS). A significant finding in the provided context is Rockwell's development of a standard 'Boiler Efficiency library' as part of the PlantPAx control system, specifically for versions 4.x through 5.x. This initiative represents a move towards providing standardized, reusable building blocks for boiler calculations, which simplifies and accelerates project implementation. This library is not just a theoretical concept; it is delivered as concrete, deployable code in the form of pre-built Function Blocks (FB) and Ladder Logic (LD) routines (.L5X files). These routines are designed to be imported directly into a Studio 5000 project, the engineering environment for ControlLogix and CompactLogix controllers, which are integral to the PlantPAx system. This design choice empowers automation engineers to configure the boiler efficiency calculation by mapping user-defined parameters to the routine's inputs. These inputs correspond to standard instrumentation signals found on a boiler, including Steam Temperature, Water Temperature, Steam Flow, Water Flow, Fuel Flow, and Steam Pressure. By providing this pre-engineered logic, Rockwell lowers the barrier to entry for implementing sophisticated efficiency calculations, ensuring a consistent and repeatable methodology across different projects and industries.

Once the raw process data is being collected by the PLCs, the next critical step is data management and contextualization. This is handled by FactoryTalk DataMosaix™, which is described as an Industrial DataOps solution. Its primary role is to act as the bridge between Operational Technology (OT) and Information Technology (IT), enabling enterprise-wide access to relevant and contextualized data. In the context of boiler OEE, DataMosaix™ aggregates and contextualizes energy data streams—such as those for steam, gas, and electricity—with production data from the shop floor. This capability is essential for performing intelligent energy assessments and identifying correlations between energy consumption and production output. For example, it allows an analyst to see how changes in boiler firing rates correlate with fluctuations in steam demand from a specific production line. This contextualization is a prerequisite for accurately calculating not just thermal efficiency, but also the broader, adapted OEE score that considers operational context.

For reporting and visualization, Rockwell offers several dedicated applications. FactoryTalk Metrics is positioned as the reporting and OEE tracking software solution. It functions as a reporting layer that generates reports and dashboards from production data already collected and stored in a database. A critical prerequisite for using FactoryTalk Metrics is having a data flow established into a historian database, such as the RA Historian SE, via the FactoryTalk Transaction Manager. This means that while the PlantPAx system can perform the real-time boiler efficiency calculation, the historical tracking and trending of this metric (and the standard OEE components) would be managed by FactoryTalk Metrics, which draws its data from the historian. This architecture separates the real-time control logic from the long-term data storage and reporting functions.

More directly aligned with the energy-centric nature of boiler performance is FactoryTalk Energy Manager™. This application is explicitly designed as an IT/OT convergence platform to monitor, analyze, and optimize energy performance across an entire enterprise. It is built upon the FactoryTalk DataMosaix™ foundation and is designed to combine energy plant models with production information at multiple levels, from site-wide down to individual machines. Its key functionalities include aggregating and contextualizing energy data, monitoring and analyzing energy performance, and providing customizable, prebuilt real-time dashboards. Given that boiler OEE is fundamentally an energy performance metric, FactoryTalk Energy Manager is the ideal platform for visualizing the results of the boiler efficiency calculations performed by the PlantPAx library. It can display trends in boiler efficiency, Steam-to-Fuel ratios, and other related energy KPIs, allowing operators and energy managers to identify inefficiencies or anomalies in near real-time.

The standard data flow architecture for FactoryTalk Metrics illustrates this layered approach. Data originates from Allen-Bradley PLCs (like ControlLogix), flows through an RSLinx Gateway, is processed by the FactoryTalk Transaction Manager, and is ultimately stored in a SQL Database Pool within the RA Historian SE. FactoryTalk Metrics then queries this historian database to generate its reports and OEE dashboards. For simpler implementations, a Logix AOI (Add-On Instruction) version of FactoryTalk Metrics exists, which integrates directly with RSLogix 5000/Studio 5000 controllers, bypassing the need for a separate transaction manager but offering less flexibility for multi-line deployments.

For a digital twin chatbot integrated with a Rockwell ecosystem, the implementation pathway is clear. The chatbot could query the real-time values from the tags associated with the Boiler Efficiency library's output in the PlantPAx system. These values, representing thermal efficiency and potentially other key parameters, would then be combined with uptime data (Availability) derived from boiler status tags. The final, calculated BOEE score, along with its constituent components, could be displayed on dashboards created within FactoryTalk Energy Manager. This platform would provide the necessary context, linking the boiler's performance to overall plant energy consumption and production targets. The existence of a standardized, pre-built library for boiler efficiency within PlantPAx significantly simplifies the technical challenge of implementing this functionality, making it a highly feasible and well-supported approach within the Rockwell Automation landscape.

## Implementation in Honeywell Process Solutions Environments

Honeywell Process Solutions (HPS) adopts a different strategic approach to providing advanced analytics and performance monitoring for assets like boilers compared to Rockwell Automation. Rather than embedding boiler-specific calculation logic directly into its flagship Experion Process Knowledge System (PKS) DCS, Honeywell focuses on delivering a unified, asset-centric platform for data management, analytics, and specialized services. This strategy positions Honeywell's ecosystem as a flexible foundation upon which customers and partners can build customized performance solutions, leveraging Honeywell's expertise in combustion and energy management.

The cornerstone of this approach is the Uniformance Suite, a collection of software solutions designed for asset-centric analytics and data management. At its core is the Uniformance® Process History Database (PHD), a product engineered to collect, store, and replay historical and continuous plant process data. The stated purpose of PHD is to enhance data security and improve process performance by providing a centralized repository for decision-making. This data management capability is the essential first step for any advanced calculation, including a boiler OEE. Before any meaningful analysis can occur, all relevant process variables—such as steam flow, temperature, pressure, fuel flow, and stack oxygen levels—must be reliably logged and accessible. The Uniformance suite provides this foundational infrastructure, as noted by its description as a unique solution in the process industry for monitoring both process and event data on a unified platform.

Within the HPS ecosystem, the Experion PKS serves as the primary DCS, integrating innovative control technologies to improve process performance. While the provided documentation does not mention a pre-packaged, off-the-shelf "Boiler Efficiency Library" analogous to Rockwell's offering, Honeywell's DCS includes features designed to directly influence boiler performance. For example, Honeywell's solutions for burner systems include a multiple screw cam assembly that provides mechanical adjustment capabilities to the air/fuel ratio at each valve position, allowing for precise control of the combustion process. Furthermore, Honeywell's combustion services and solutions emphasize optimizing operational parameters like furnace draft pressure, which is critical for stable and efficient combustion. This suggests that Honeywell's philosophy is to equip operators with superior control and visibility into the underlying processes that drive efficiency, rather than automating the final OEE calculation itself.

For the actual calculation of boiler efficiency and the subsequent creation of a boiler OEE score, the implementation in a Honeywell environment would likely be more bespoke. A developer or engineer would utilize the Uniformance PHD to log the necessary process data. With this data securely stored, they could then develop a custom calculation. This could involve writing scripts, developing logic within the Experion PKS itself, or using Honeywell's own analytics tools. The Experion GMS User Manual mentions configuring trackers and notifications, which implies the system has the capability to monitor key variables and trigger actions based on their values, a necessary feature for calculating an availability score. The calculation of thermal efficiency would require implementing the appropriate formula (direct or indirect method) using the logged data for fuel input and steam output.

To visualize and report on the resulting boiler OEE score, Honeywell provides dedicated energy management solutions. Honeywell offers a product called Energy Management Software that is designed to provide near real-time visibility and predictive analytics for energy performance. This software is built to monitor, control, and enhance energy performance, aligning perfectly with the needs of a boiler-focused analysis. It enables users to track consumption, intensity, costs, and even emissions, and critically, it allows for the contextualization of energy data with production data to identify inefficiencies. This makes Honeywell's Energy Management Software the natural platform for displaying the final BOEE score and its components, similar to how FactoryTalk Energy Manager serves this role in the Rockwell ecosystem.

Honeywell's strategy also heavily emphasizes domain expertise through its combustion safety and optimization services. Their focus is on saving lives, preventing explosions, and increasing the efficiency and reliability of combustion equipment. This service-oriented approach complements their software offerings. For a facility looking to improve boiler performance, Honeywell can provide not just the tools for measurement but also the expert consultation to interpret the data and implement changes, such as optimizing the air/fuel ratio or managing furnace draft pressure. This combination of robust data management platforms, advanced control systems, and specialized services creates a comprehensive solution for improving boiler performance, even if the path to calculating a formal "boiler OEE" requires more custom development than with some competitors.

For a digital twin chatbot interfacing with a Honeywell environment, the implementation would involve a few key steps. First, the chatbot would need to be connected to the Experion PKS or Uniformance PHD to access real-time and historical process data. Second, since a pre-built boiler efficiency calculation is not explicitly mentioned, a custom calculation module would need to be developed and deployed. This module would ingest the necessary sensor data to compute thermal efficiency and availability. Finally, the output of this custom calculation would be fed into a dashboarding tool, likely within the Experion PKS interface or using Honeywell's dedicated Energy Management Software, to present the BOEE score and its components to the user. The chatbot could then query this dashboard or the underlying calculation module to provide answers to user questions about boiler performance.

## Synthesis and Recommendations for Digital Twin Integration

The comprehensive analysis of generalized OEE methodologies and their adaptations for boiler systems reveals a clear pathway for implementing a relevant performance metric within industrial automation platforms like Rockwell and Honeywell. The core insight is that a direct application of the discrete-manufacturing OEE formula (Availability × Performance × Quality) is inadequate for a continuous-process asset like a boiler. Instead, the framework must evolve into a hybrid model that prioritizes energy efficiency alongside operational uptime. This synthesized approach provides the most practical and insightful basis for a digital twin chatbot designed to answer questions about boiler performance.

The recommended approach is to define a new metric, tentatively named Boiler Overall Energy Effectiveness (BOEE), calculated using a formula that mirrors the structure of traditional OEE but incorporates boiler-specific KPIs:

$$\text{BOEE} = \text{Availability} \times \text{Thermal Efficiency} \times \text{Steam Quality Factor}$$

This formula explicitly combines three critical dimensions of boiler performance:
1. **Availability:** This component retains its standard definition, measuring the uptime percentage of the boiler during its scheduled run time. It is a simple yet vital indicator of the asset's reliability and its ability to consistently supply steam to the process. A low availability score immediately flags issues with equipment reliability or maintenance planning.
2. **Thermal Efficiency:** This replaces the generic "Performance" component. It quantifies the boiler's effectiveness in converting fuel energy into usable steam energy. This metric is the primary driver of operational cost and environmental impact. Calculating it requires real-time data on fuel input and steam output, and it can be expressed as a percentage of the boiler's rated or optimal efficiency. Improvements in this area directly translate to fuel savings.
3. **Steam Quality Factor:** This adapts the "Quality" concept for a boiler. Since a boiler doesn't produce discrete "good" or "bad" parts, quality is redefined as the consistency and adherence of the steam's key parameters (pressure, temperature, dryness) to their setpoints. This factor could be a proxy calculated from the variance of these parameters over a period. Stable, predictable steam output is a hallmark of a well-tuned and healthy boiler, and instability can be considered a quality loss affecting downstream processes.

To power this BOEE calculation for a digital twin chatbot, access to a specific set of real-time process data is non-negotiable. The chatbot's backend system must be able to query these data streams from the plant's DCS or historian. The essential real-time inputs are:
- **Operational Status:** A tag indicating whether the boiler is Running, Standby, or Down. This is used to calculate the Availability component.
- **Steam Generation Parameters:** Real-time measurements of Steam Flow Rate, Steam Pressure, and Steam Temperature.
- **Feedwater Parameters:** Real-time measurements of Feedwater Flow Rate and Temperature, which are necessary for a precise thermal balance calculation.
- **Fuel Input Parameters:** Real-time measurements of Fuel Flow Rate (for gas, oil, or coal) and potentially Fuel Temperature.
- **Combustion Parameters:** Real-time Stack Oxygen levels, which are a direct indicator of combustion efficiency and excess air. Furnace Draft Pressure is also a critical parameter for stable combustion.
- **Water Chemistry (Optional but Recommended):** Blowdown TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) readings, as high levels can indicate excessive blowdown and energy loss.

The implementation of this logic differs between the Rockwell and Honeywell ecosystems. In a Rockwell Automation environment, the path is streamlined by the existence of a pre-built 'Boiler Efficiency library'. The chatbot could leverage this library for the real-time thermal efficiency calculation. The availability component can be calculated independently from status tags. The results can then be visualized and trended using FactoryTalk Energy Manager™, which is purpose-built for contextualizing energy data with production information. This provides a turnkey solution for implementing the proposed BOEE metric.

In a Honeywell Process Solutions environment, the approach is more flexible but requires a greater degree of customization. There is no indication of a pre-packaged boiler efficiency library. Therefore, a custom calculation module would need to be developed. This module would reside within the Experion PKS or be executed using an analytics tool, drawing its data from the Uniformance® Process History Database (PHD). The final BOEE score and its components would then be presented to the user via dashboards within Honeywell's Energy Management Software or the Experion PKS itself. While this requires more initial development effort, it offers the advantage of being tightly tailored to the specific characteristics of the plant's boilers.

The ultimate value of the digital twin chatbot lies not just in providing a numerical score, but in delivering contextual, actionable insights. When a user asks, "What is my boiler's OEE?", the response should be a narrative that breaks down the score. For example: "Your BOEE today is 75%. Your Availability is strong at 95%, but your Thermal Efficiency is holding you back at 80%. The primary cause appears to be high stack oxygen levels, which suggest you are using excess air. Reducing the oxygen trim could improve your efficiency and cut fuel costs." This type of response transforms the BOEE from a passive metric into an active diagnostic tool. It connects the score directly to specific operational levers that the user can pull, such as adjusting the air/fuel ratio or investigating a reason for frequent startups. By integrating the BOEE calculation with other key operational data—like the Steam-to-Fuel ratio, blowdown TDS, and emissions—the chatbot can provide a truly holistic view of boiler health, empowering operators to make data-driven decisions that enhance both performance and profitability.
